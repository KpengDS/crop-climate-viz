How Do Seasons Shape Crops & Climate?

This visualization explores how land surface temperature (LST), precipitation, and vegetation health (NDVI) interact seasonally across three of America's most important crop-producing regions — Iowa (corn), Kansas (wheat), and Texas (cotton).

Research Question

How do seasonal patterns of precipitation, land surface temperature (LST), and vegetation dynamics (NDVI) interact across major crop-producing regions in Iowa (corn), Kansas (wheat), and Texas (cotton)?

Write-up

What have we done so far?

We extracted monthly MODIS satellite data (NDVI, LST Day/Night) and CHIRPS precipitation data for Iowa, Kansas, and Texas using Google Earth Engine. We built a D3.js interactive webpage with four visualization sections: a US map highlighting the three states, choropleth color maps with a month slider, multi-line seasonal trend charts with variable switching, and scatterplots showing correlations between climate and vegetation variables.

What will be most challenging to design?

The most challenging part will be the choropleth maps at county or sub-state resolution, which requires additional spatial data beyond current state-level averages. Accurately mapping variation within each state demands raster-to-vector aggregation at finer resolution. Balancing visual clarity across two side-by-side maps while keeping the time slider intuitive will also require careful layout and color scale decisions.

Data Sources

NASA MODIS products: MOD13A2 (NDVI), MOD11A2 (LST Day), MYD11A2 (LST Night), MCD12Q1 (Land Cover). Precipitation from UCSB-CHG/CHIRPS/DAILY. All data extracted via Google Earth Engine for the years 2015–2020, averaged monthly across the three target states.

Visual Encoding

Choropleth maps use sequential color scales: green for NDVI, orange for LST, and blue for precipitation. Line charts use distinct colors per state — yellow for Iowa, green for Kansas, red-orange for Texas — consistent across all visualizations. Scatter plots encode month as dot position and state as color.
