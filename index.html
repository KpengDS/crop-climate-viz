Research Question

How do seasonal patterns of precipitation, land surface temperature (LST), and vegetation dynamics (NDVI) interact across major crop-producing regions in Iowa (corn), Kansas (wheat), and Texas (cotton)?

Section 05

What We Found

The story of America's agricultural heartland unfolds in satellite data. What does it tell us about the future?

What have we done so far?

We extracted monthly MODIS satellite data (NDVI, LST Day/Night) and CHIRPS precipitation data for Iowa, Kansas, and Texas using Google Earth Engine, enabling year-round monitoring of key climate and vegetation indicators. We built a US map that highlights the three states with hover tooltips displaying average climate metrics, providing geographic context for the analysis. We created side-by-side choropleth maps with a month slider and variable switching controls, allowing users to compare LST Day, LST Night, and precipitation across all three states for any given month. Finally, we built multi-line seasonal trend charts and scatter plots with interactive tooltips to reveal how NDVI, LST, and precipitation interact across Iowa, Kansas, and Texas throughout the year.

What will be most challenging to design?

Transitioning to sub-state choropleth maps at the county level will be our biggest challenge, requiring substantial county-level raster and vector data that goes beyond our current state-level aggregations. The raster-to-vector aggregation process itself is technically complex — correctly transforming satellite raster data into meaningful county boundaries without distorting or losing spatial information demands careful handling. Balancing two side-by-side interactive maps with distinct color scales while keeping the month slider and variable selector responsive and intuitive will require thoughtful layout and careful performance optimization. Finally, ensuring that all interactions — tooltips, hover states, filtering, and cross-chart synchronization — remain bug-free and cohesive across all four visualization types is essential to delivering an intuitive user experience. Moreover, the creative way of telling the clear story of the research topic for our project by scrollytelling is also a challenging part.

Data Sources

NASA MODIS products: MOD13A2 (NDVI), MOD11A2 (LST Day), MYD11A2 (LST Night), MCD12Q1 (Land Cover). Precipitation from UCSB-CHG/CHIRPS/DAILY. All data extracted via Google Earth Engine for the years 2015–2020, averaged monthly across the three target states.

Visual Encoding

Choropleth maps use sequential color scales: green for NDVI, orange for LST, and blue for precipitation. Line charts use distinct colors per state — yellow for Iowa, green for Kansas, red-orange for Texas — consistent across all visualizations. Scatter plots encode month as dot position and state as color.
