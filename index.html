How Do Climate Shape Crop Production?

This visualization explores the data in 2023 of how land surface temperature (LST), precipitation, correlate with vegetation health (NDVI) seasonally across three of America's most important crop-producing regions — Iowa (corn), Kansas (wheat), and Texas (cotton).

NVDI is a remote sensing metric that measures the health, density, and greenness of vegetation. It is directly correlated with plant health and biomass: we usually have low NVDI for drought, pest, nutrient deficiencies of crops. For tracking crop growth, NVDI values rise from emergence (0.2–0.4) to peak greenness (0.6–0.8), and decline during natural aging prior to harvest. We also monitor NDVI peaks and anomalies for crop yield prediction. (EOS Data Analytics)

Research Question

How do seasonal patterns of precipitation and land surface temperature (LST) correlate with normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) across major crop-producing regions in Iowa (corn), Kansas (wheat), and Texas (cotton)?

Write-up

What have we done so far?

We first use TIGER to restrict the data into specific states that are the focus for our research: Iowa, Kansas and Texas. Then we define the start time January and end time December in 2023 to extract NVDI and LST data from NASA MODIS and calculate average NVDI, LST day and night for each month. Next we extract data from CHIRPS to calculate total precipitation data for Iowa, Kansas, and Texas using Google Earth Engine. We built a D3.js interactive webpage with four visualization sections: a US map highlighting the three states, choropleth color maps with a month slider, multi-line seasonal trend charts with variable switching, and scatterplots showing correlations between climate and vegetation variables.

What will be most challenging to design?

The most challenging part will be the choropleth maps at county or sub-state resolution, which requires additional spatial data beyond current state-level averages. Accurately mapping variation within each state demands raster-to-vector aggregation at finer resolution. Balancing visual clarity across two side-by-side maps while keeping the time slider intuitive will also require careful layout and color scale decisions.

Data Sources

TIGER/2018/States for states data. NASA MODIS products: MOD13A2 (NDVI), MOD11A2 (LST Day), MYD11A2 (LST Night), MCD12Q1 (Land Cover). Precipitation from UCSB-CHG/CHIRPS/DAILY. All data extracted via Google Earth Engine for the years 2015–2020, averaged monthly across the three target states.

Visual Encoding

Choropleth maps use sequential color scales: green for NDVI, orange for LST, and blue for precipitation. Line charts use distinct colors per state — yellow for Iowa, green for Kansas, red-orange for Texas — consistent across all visualizations. Scatter plots encode month as dot position and state as color.
